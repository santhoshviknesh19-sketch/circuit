What is Ohm's Law?

Ohm's Law is a fundamental principle in electronics that describes the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in an electrical circuit. It was named after Georg Ohm, a German physicist who discovered this relationship in 1827.

The law states that the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage applied across it, provided the temperature remains constant. In other words, if you increase the voltage, the current increases proportionally, and if you increase the resistance, the current decreases.

The Mathematical Formula

The formula for Ohm's Law is:

V = I × R

Where:

  • V is the voltage (in volts)
  • I is the current (in amperes)
  • R is the resistance (in ohms)

Units of Measurement

The standard units used in Ohm's Law are:

  • Voltage: Volts (V) - measures electrical potential difference
  • Current: Amperes (A) - measures the flow of electric charge
  • Resistance: Ohms (Ω) - measures opposition to current flow

Practical Applications

Ohm's Law is essential for designing and analyzing electrical circuits. It helps engineers calculate the appropriate resistor values, determine power requirements, and troubleshoot circuit problems.
