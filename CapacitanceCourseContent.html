Capacitance Course

Master the fundamental concepts of capacitance and capacitor behavior in electrical circuits.

What is Capacitance?

Capacitance is the ability of a capacitor to store electrical charge. A capacitor consists of two conductive plates separated by an insulating material called a dielectric. When a voltage is applied across the plates, positive charge accumulates on one plate and negative charge on the other.

Figure 1: Complete capacitor charging circuit showing charge accumulation on plates

The capacitance of a capacitor depends on the area of the plates, the distance between them, and the dielectric constant of the material between them. Capacitors are fundamental components in electronics used for energy storage, filtering, timing, and signal processing.

The Mathematical Formula

The basic formula for capacitance is:

C = Q/V

Where:

  • C is the capacitance (in farads, F)
  • Q is the charge stored (in coulombs, C)
  • V is the voltage across the capacitor (in volts, V)

Capacitor Combinations

When capacitors are connected in series:

1/C_eq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ... + 1/Cₙ

Figure 2: Capacitors connected in series - voltage divides, current is the same

When capacitors are connected in parallel:

C_eq = C₁ + C₂ + ... + Cₙ

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

The energy stored in a charged capacitor is:

E = (1/2)CV²

or equivalently:

E = (1/2)QV

E = Q²/(2C)

Figure 4: Energy storage in a capacitor - electrical energy is stored in the electric field between plates

Units of Measurement

The standard units used with capacitors are:

  • Capacitance: Farads (F) - the SI unit, though practical capacitors use μF, nF, pF
  • Charge: Coulombs (C) - measures stored electrical charge
  • Voltage: Volts (V) - measures electrical potential difference
  • Energy: Joules (J) - measures stored electrical energy

Practical Applications

Capacitors have numerous applications in electronics:

  • Energy Storage: Storing electrical energy for later use
  • Filtering: Removing noise and smoothing power supplies
  • Timing: Creating time delays in RC circuits
  • Coupling: Passing AC signals while blocking DC
  • Tuning: Selecting specific frequencies in radio circuits
